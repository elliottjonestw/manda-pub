How to Improve Your Chinese Reading Speed

The Manda Team · July 12, 2026 · 9 min read

If you've learned a few hundred characters and can technically read a paragraph of Chinese, you've probably hit the same wall everyone does: you read slowly. Painfully slowly. Your eyes stall, back up, re-parse where one word ends and the next begins, and by the time you reach the end of a sentence you've forgotten how it started. Reading feels like decoding, not reading.

Speed is not a vanity metric here. Slow reading is a comprehension problem in disguise: if decoding eats all your attention, there's nothing left over to actually understand the text. This article looks at what the research really says about reading faster, why Chinese is a special case, and how Manda's Speed Reading mode is built around the parts that hold up.

First, an honest word about "speed reading"

The internet is full of promises that you can learn to read 1,000+ words per minute with full comprehension. You mostly can't, and it's worth saying so up front.

The most cited review of the field, Rayner and colleagues' 2016 paper "So Much to Read, So Little Time", concludes that there is a real, stubborn trade-off between speed and comprehension. You can move your eyes faster, but past a certain point you're trading away understanding to do it. There's no free lunch where you double your speed and keep all the meaning.

So the goal isn't to become a superhuman skimmer. The goal is more modest and more achievable: to close the gap between how fast you decode and how fast you could comprehend, to stop being the bottleneck in your own reading.

Why you read Chinese slowly

Reading researchers have spent decades tracking readers' eyes, and slow reading tends to come from a few specific habits:

  • Subvocalization: silently "saying" each word in your head. This is useful for memory, but it chains your reading speed to your speaking speed, roughly 150 words per minute. For a learner sounding out unfamiliar characters, it's even slower.
  • Regressions: flicking your eyes backward to re-read. These are a normal part of reading (10–15% of eye movements even in fluent readers), but for learners they balloon: you re-read because you're unsure, and the re-reading itself breaks your flow.
  • Inefficient fixations: your eyes jump and pause, jump and pause. A beginner in a new script pauses more often and takes in fewer characters per pause.

Now add the twist that makes Chinese uniquely hard.

The Chinese problem: where does the word end?

English gives you spaces. Chinese doesn't. The characters run together (我们今天去图书馆看书) and part of skilled reading is silently deciding where one word stops and the next begins. Native readers do this effortlessly; skilled L1 Chinese readers hit around 250 words per minute, comparable to English readers.

Learners don't have that reflex yet. Research on second-language Chinese readers finds they have a smaller perceptual span (they take in fewer characters per glance) and fall back on a slow, one-character-at-a-time segmentation strategy, testing each boundary as they go. Studies consistently find that word-segmentation accuracy and character-naming speed are among the strongest predictors of reading comprehension in L2 Chinese. In plain terms: a huge amount of your reading effort is spent just figuring out where the words are. Only what's left over goes toward meaning.

This is exactly why a tool that shows you the words, pre-segmented, is more useful for Chinese than it is for English.

What actually builds reading speed

Here the language-teaching research is much more encouraging than the "speed reading" hype. Two well-studied techniques genuinely work for second-language learners:

  • Timed reading: reading level-appropriate texts against the clock, tracking your speed over time. In controlled studies, learners doing timed reading raised their reading rate by around 25% (e.g. from ~118 to ~147 wpm) while control groups barely moved.
  • Repeated reading: reading the same passage several times to build fluency, with gains that transfer to new, unseen texts.

The applied-linguist Paul Nation argues that fluency deserves to be a core strand of any reading course, and that with vocabulary-controlled material, learners can realistically build toward 200–250 wpm. The two non-negotiables in all of this research are: the text must be at the right level (you already know almost all the words), and you have to do it regularly.

Notice what these methods have in common: a gentle, consistent push slightly past your comfortable pace, on material you can actually handle. That's the lever.

Where RSVP fits in

RSVP, or Rapid Serial Visual Presentation, flashes words one at a time in a single fixed spot on the screen, so you never move your eyes. It's the mechanism behind apps like Spritz, and it's what powers Manda's Speed Reading mode.

RSVP is not magic, and it's fair to be skeptical: because the words vanish, you lose the ability to make regressions and to preview the next word, and the 2016 research shows this hurts comprehension at very high speeds. But that same research points to a sweet spot. Studies comparing RSVP to normal reading found no significant comprehension loss at around 250–350 wpm; the drop only shows up when people crank the speed far higher.

Used sensibly, RSVP does three genuinely helpful things for a learner:

  1. It removes the eye-movement inefficiency. No stalling, no drifting, no regressions; the word is simply there, then gone.
  2. It gives you a built-in metronome for timed reading. Setting a words-per-minute target is timed reading, automated.
  3. For Chinese specifically, it solves the segmentation problem for you. Each flash is one word, already parsed, so you can put your effort into meaning instead of hunting for boundaries.

That last point is the one most people miss. In English, RSVP is a convenience. In Chinese, presenting one segmented word at a time removes the single biggest thing standing between a learner and fluent reading.

How Manda's Speed Reading mode is designed

Manda's Speed Reading mode is built to lean on what the research supports and avoid what it warns against.

  • Real word segmentation, not character-by-character. Manda runs the text through the same Jieba word-segmentation engine it uses across the app, so each flash is a genuine word (你好, 图书馆, 已经), not a stream of isolated characters. You're training the exact skill L2 readers are weakest at.
  • Graded, level-appropriate texts. You read from Manda's graded Stories/Articles and News libraries, filtered by HSK or TOCFL level. That satisfies the research's first rule: read material at the right level.
  • Sensible speeds, front and centre. Presets run 120–400 wpm, right around the evidence-based sweet spot, and you can nudge the pace up or down mid-session (↑/↓ keys). Start where comprehension is easy, then push gently; that's the timed-reading method in action.
  • Natural rhythm, not a rigid metronome. Longer words linger a little, and Manda inserts short pauses at commas and longer ones at full stops (，。！？), so the flow reads like language and gives you a beat to process each clause.
  • Optional scaffolding. Toggle pinyin above each word while you're still shaky, then turn it off to wean yourself onto the characters alone. Switch between Simplified and Traditional, and pick a font you find easy to read.
  • A speed you can actually track. At the end, Manda shows your word count, time, and effective words-per-minute, so you can do the one thing the research says matters most: come back and beat it. Re-reading a story you've already done is repeated reading; doing a new one at your target pace is timed reading.

None of this is a shortcut around learning vocabulary: if you don't know the words, no presentation speed will save you. But once you do know most of the words in a text, Speed Reading is a focused way to turn slow, effortful decoding into smooth, fluent reading.

A practical routine

If you want to actually move the needle, try this:

  1. Pick a story one level below where you struggle, so comprehension should feel easy.
  2. Set the speed to something comfortable (say 150 wpm) and read it once, pinyin on.
  3. Read it again at the same speed with pinyin off.
  4. Bump the speed up 20 wpm and read a new story at the same level.
  5. Do this a few times a week. Watch your comfortable speed climb.

The gains in the research came from consistency, not intensity. Ten focused minutes several times a week will do far more than one heroic hour.

The bottom line

You can't cheat the speed–comprehension trade-off, and anyone selling 1,000-wpm superpowers is overselling. But the boring, evidence-backed truth is genuinely good news: with level-appropriate texts, a gentle push past your comfortable pace, and regular practice, second-language readers reliably get faster. For Chinese, where so much effort goes into just finding the word boundaries, seeing the words pre-segmented and one at a time is an especially good fit.
